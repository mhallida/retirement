How any of this works

Why everything is in today's dollars

A 19-year-old looking at "$200,000 a year in 2065 dollars" is staring at a number that's been mangled by decades of inflation. It doesn't feel like anything.

So this tool uses real returns — the part of the return that's left after inflation. The dollar amounts you see represent the same purchasing power as today. If you want the inflated nominal numbers anyway, toggle the option in the inputs.

Where 7% comes from

The U.S. stock market has returned roughly 10% per year on average over the last century. Inflation has run around 3%. The difference — about 7% — is the real return: the part that actually grows your purchasing power.

Past performance is not a promise. Markets can underperform for a decade or more. But over 40-year horizons, the spread between equities and inflation has been remarkably stable.

The 4% rule

The widely-cited "4% rule" says a retiree can withdraw 4% of their starting balance each year, adjusted for inflation, and have a high probability of not running out of money over a 30-year retirement.

It implies a balanced portfolio earning roughly 4% real return during drawdown, which is the default here. Adjust it down if you plan to be more conservative or live longer.

What compounding actually is

Compounding is when your gains start earning their own gains. A dollar invested at 7% real return doesn't just grow — it accelerates.

$1 → $2 → $4 → $8 → $16 → $32 → $64 → $128

That's $1 doubling about every ten years at 7%. Forty years of compounding turns a dollar into roughly $15. Twenty years turns it into about $4. The early years are where the magic builds — which is why when you start matters more than how much you contribute.

What this doesn't include

A lot. This is a teaching tool, not a financial plan. It doesn't model:

  • Taxes (contributions and withdrawals are taxed very differently across account types)
  • Social Security (will likely cover a meaningful chunk of your retirement income)
  • Employer 401(k) match (free money — do not leave it on the table)
  • Sequence-of-returns risk (a bad decade right after you retire is much worse than the same bad decade in the middle)
  • Rising contributions as your income grows (most people contribute more in their forties than in their twenties)

The point isn't precision. It's to show, in one chart, what waiting actually costs.
